Roswell's Unique Air Quality Concerns

Roswell's historic district is exceptional — and that history comes with real air quality responsibilities. Antebellum-era and Victorian homes built in the 1800s and early 1900s almost certainly contain lead-based paint in original layers beneath decades of repainting. These properties were also constructed with materials and methods that predate modern building codes, meaning moisture management was never a design priority. Older masonry foundations, unventilated crawl spaces, and aging roof assemblies create pathways for moisture intrusion that fuel mold growth inside walls, under floors, and in attic spaces.

The Roswell Mill complex and surrounding structures represent a category of historic adaptive reuse where asbestos testing is particularly critical. Original industrial buildings often contain asbestos in roofing, insulation, pipe lagging, and structural fireproofing. Any renovation, demolition, or change of use in these structures requires thorough asbestos survey and testing to protect workers and comply with EPA National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP).

Even Roswell's 1970s through 1990s subdivisions are now old enough to raise concerns. Homes built before 1978 contain lead paint, and many 1970s-era homes contain asbestos in vinyl floor tiles, acoustic ceiling texture, and duct tape around HVAC systems. The humid Georgia climate adds ongoing mold pressure — particularly in properties near the Chattahoochee River National Recreation Area, where elevated moisture levels in the surrounding environment can migrate into homes through inadequately sealed foundations and crawl spaces.
